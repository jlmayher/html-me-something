HTML Me Something: A Story

Isn't a story just a series of events?

Stories are not merely a string of events. Stories have structure and elements that make them distinct from a simple description of things that happened. The events of a story are woven together in a such a way that tension is built up during the beginning and middle with a rapid decrease in the amount of tension after the climax and denouement.

    Major Elements of a story

  • Beginning
  • Middle
  • Climax
  • Denouement
  • End

Let's look at two different pieces

A Thanksgiving Day Hike

On Thanksgiving Day in 2016, my husband, Philip, and I decided to have breakfast by Shady Lake in Cuivre River State Park. The afternoon was full of family events, so we thought it would be nice to go for a hike and have breakfast with just the two of us at one of our favorite spots. It was going to be tight time-wise since it would be about 3 hours for the hike and breakfast and we had to be back in time for showers before heading off to my in-laws's; for late lunch.

The weather was a bit wet that morning, but the park was so peaceful that it just added to the ambience. We didn't pass another person in the mile and a half down the Lone Spring Trail to Shady 80 Lake. It was in the 40s that day, perfect weather for a fall hike. There were deer and a few squirrels out and about, but not people. I guess everyone was back at home with their families, leaving us to enjoy the scene undisturbed.

When we got to the lake we set about making breakfast. Philip got out the stove and we put water on to boil while I readied the food packages for prep. We picked out our favorite freeze-dried breakfast meal, Mountain House Scrambled Eggs with Ham and Peppers for that morning because it would be quick and easy to prepare and didnâ€™t require lugging complex cooking apparatus with us. Just a pot, two bowls, a small stove and white gas canister were all we needed.

Originally, our plan was to hike all 5.2 miles plus the half-mile round trip to the lake, but, like many other people, we had afternoon plans for dinner with family and had to take a connector to cut a mile and a half off of the trail. We enjoyed our holiday hike so much we went to Castlewood State Park for New Year's Eve.

My Brother to the Rescue

I was at Nick's Bar-B-Q and Catfish in Carlyle, Arkansas with my parents and brother. Everytime we go down to visit family and friends we stop at Nick's in Mom's home town. They have the best fried catfish you can find anywhere, and you can top off your dinner with a fried pie. I'm nearly willing to drive 7 hours down there just to have some of that catfish. My family had just finished eating and were waiting up front to pay. I said to my mother,
"I'm going to the restroom. Don't leave without me."

This is the beginning of the story. Here we introduce our setting and characters. We end the beginning with the event that causes the rise in tension.


When I came out of the restroom, my family was gone. I thought to myself "Not a big deal. They're probably just waiting outside." I went outside, looked around, and didn't see them. Then, I saw them. Dad was driving down the road in the truck, My mother and brother we just leaving the parking lot in the car. Crap.

I ran through the parking lot, heedless of the cars driving through. The only thing I could think was that I had to get Mom's attention or be stuck here. Unfortunately for me, she didn't notice me jumping up and down in the road in her rear-view mirror, and continued to drive off to my grandmother's house.

Now I was worried. This was long enough ago that we didn't have cell phones that worked out of state, and the phone service to my grandmother's house had been turned off when she moved to the assisted-care facility. I really didn't know who to call. Our cousin lived next door to grandma's house, but even though I'd know her my entire life, I had no idea what her last name was (Thomas, I found out later it was Thomas). I slumped onto the bench in front of Nick's trying to decide if I should ask someone there for a ride out to my grandmother's house or try to walk the several miles there. Could I call the police? I was an adult, not a lost child. What would they say? Just as I was about to walk inside and talk to someone, a familiar looking 1994 Chrysler New Yorker pulled up, rolled down the window and from inside someone called,

"Need a ride somewhere?"

Through the middle section the tension rises as the protagonist encounters a series of obstacles to reuniting with her family.

It was my mother! I was saved from having to ask a total stranger for help because I didn't know my own cousin's last name. I very nearly fainted from relief. It turned out that both of my parents assumed I was riding with the other and the only reason Mom returned was that my brother saw me jumping up and down in the road and asked Mom who I was riding with. When she told him I was with Dad, he said,

"If she's riding with Dad, then why is she jumping up and down in the road behind us?"

One would think this was the last time my parents nearly left me alone in a place far from home, but not everone learns their lesson quickly. It wasn't until Dad tried to drive off while I was actually getting into the car in Washington, DC that they started paying more attention.

In the end of the story, we have the denouement where the resolution to the story arc is explained.

Why is "My Brother to the Rescue" a story and "Thanksgiving Day Hike" is not?

Without a buildup of tension and the resolution of the tension after the climax, Thanksgiving Day Hike just relates a series of events. However, by constructing a narrative arc "My Brother to the Rescue" tells a story of the protagonist being left behind by her family, the tension builds as she considers whether she has any way to contact them or will she be left waiting and finally the resolution occurs when her mother and brother return to the restaurant to retrieve her.

The structure creates a story out of a series of events
